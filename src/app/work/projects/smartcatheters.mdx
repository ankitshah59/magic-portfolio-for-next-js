## Executive Summary

We present a **height-compensated rotary 3D printing platform** that directly writes electronic sensors and functional components onto soft, medical-grade catheters. This system transforms passive tubes into intelligent medical devices capable of **real-time physiological monitoring**, **localized therapy delivery**, and **secure blockchain-based tracking**.

## The Problem: Why Current Catheters Fall Short

### Clinical Need

Catheters are foundational tools in minimally invasive medicine:
- **Cardiac catheterization**: Coronary artery evaluation
- **Vascular surgery**: Stent placement
- **Oncology**: Localized drug delivery
- **Urology**: Drainage and monitoring

**Critical Gap**: Most catheters remain fundamentally **passive conduits** lacking sensing or actuating functions, limiting their ability to provide real-time feedback or adapt to physiological conditions.

### Limitations of Current Approaches

**Traditional Planar Microfabrication:**
- Electronics fabricated on flat films via photolithography
- Films wrapped or bonded onto curved catheter surfaces
- **Major problems:**
  - Delamination during bending/inflation
  - Buckling and creasing under strain
  - Stress concentrations causing sensor failure
  - Poor conformity to small-diameter catheters (2-6 mm)
  - Intermediate adhesives create failure points
  - Labor-intensive multi-step assembly
  - Difficult to scale for manufacturing

**Existing Printed Solutions:**
- **Aerosol jet printing**: Non-contact but requires specialized atomizers, limited material range
- **Inkjet printing**: Fine features but struggles with high-viscosity inks
- **Open-loop systems**: Assume idealized cylindrical substrates, fail with real-world irregularities
- **Commercial 6-axis robots**: Complex setup, limited to 180¬∞ surface coverage, requires rigid substrates

**The Core Issue**: No practical system exists for conformal, high-fidelity printing on flexible, deformable cylindrical medical devices.

## Our Solution: Closed-Loop Rotary Printing

### System Architecture

**Motion Control:**
- **Theta-axis (PRM1Z8)**: Precision rotation stage (0.0008¬∞ resolution) replaces Y-axis
- **X-axis (LTS150)**: Longitudinal translation (¬±1 Œºm accuracy)
- **Z-axis (LTS150)**: Real-time height adjustment with PID control
- **Cylindrical coordinate transformation**: Œ∏-rotation synchronized with X-Z motion

**Real-Time Height Sensing:**
- **Keyence CL-3000 confocal sensor**: 10 kHz sampling rate, 0.25 Œºm resolution
- Positioned 5 mm ahead of print nozzle
- Continuously scans surface topography during printing
- Detects bumps, bends, diameter changes in real-time

**Closed-Loop Feedback:**
- PID controller adjusts Z-axis to maintain constant 50 Œºm standoff
- Response time: Less than 80 ms with less than 2 Œºm overshoot
- Compensates for ¬±100 Œºm height variations without disrupting print
- Accounts for catheter sagging, eccentricity, balloon inflation dynamics

**Pneumatic Extrusion:**
- Nordson EFD Ultimus V dispenser (0-800 kPa, 1 kPa resolution)
- Compatible with viscosities: 150-5000 cP
- Interchangeable nozzles: 21-32 gauge (customizable line width)

**LIMR Software** (Lathe-based Intelligent Microdispensing and Registration):
- Custom C# interface for cylindrical G-code interpretation
- Real-time motion synchronization across all axes
- Automated surface pre-scanning and height mapping
- Material presets for different inks

## Materials and Inks

### Conductive Inks

**1. Silver-TPU (Ag-TPU) Composite**

**Formulation:**
- 14 g silver flakes + 1.5 g TPU pellets (Elastollan 60A)
- Solvents: 3.8 mL THF + 4 mL DMF
- Mixing: Planetary centrifugal mixer (2000 rpm, 5 min)

**Processing:**
- Intense pulse sintering: 22 J/cm¬≤ for 10 min
- Thermal curing: 80¬∞C for 10 min
- Enhanced conductivity after processing

**Properties:**
- Viscosity: 1000-3000 cP
- Stretchable and flexible
- Good adhesion to silicone and TPU substrates
- Suitable for microheaters and interconnects

**2. Silver Nanoparticle Ink**

**Commercial source**: Sigma-Aldrich, Nova Centrix PSI-211
- Particle size: approximately 20 nm
- Conductivity: 38% of bulk silver
- Curing: 80¬∞C
- **Applications**: High-resolution features, RFID resonators
- **Note**: Future recommendation to use copper or gold nanoparticle inks for higher conductivity (10‚Å∂-10‚Å∑ S/m)

**3. CNT/Ecoflex Composite (Piezoresistive)**

**Formulation:**
- Multi-wall carbon nanotubes (MWCNT): 0.5% w/v

**Properties:**
- Highly stretchable (greater than 100% strain)

- Excellent mechanical durability

**Formulation:**
- PEDOT:PSS dispersion (Clevios PH1000)

### 2. Temperature Sensors

**Design**: Interdigitated capacitor (IDC) with PEDOT:PSS+CNT overlay

**Structure:**
- **Layer 1**: Ag-TPU IDC electrodes (9 fingers, 4.5 mm long, 0.2 mm wide, 0.5 mm spacing)
- **Layer 2**: PEDOT:PSS+CNT composite (8√ó5 mm patch)
- Active area: 7√ó10 mm on catheter surface
- Compatible with 3 mm and 6 mm diameter catheters

**Sensing Mechanism:**
- Negative temperature coefficient of resistance (TCR)
- Temperature increase ‚Üí Resistance decrease
- **Physical basis**: 
  - Improved PEDOT chain ordering at higher temperatures
  - Enhanced CNT junction conductivity via thermal expansion
  - Better charge carrier tunneling

**Performance:**
- **TCR**: -0.583% per ¬∞C
- **Temperature range**: 25-45¬∞C (physiological)
- **Linearity**: R¬≤ greater than 0.9995
- **Resolution**: Better than 0.1¬∞C
- **Response time**: 
  - Heating: 90% of final value in approximately 12 seconds
  - Cooling: Return to baseline in approximately 27 seconds
- **Stability**: Negligible drift over 5 heating-cooling cycles
- **Device-to-device variation**: Standard deviation less than 0.5%

**Applications:**
- Thermal ablation monitoring
- Hot saline infusion temperature control
- Infection detection (fever in situ)
- Hyperthermia treatment feedback
- Tissue perfusion assessment

### 4. Chipless RFID Tags

**Design Concept**: Passive wireless identification via resonant structures

**No microchip required**: Resonant conductive patterns encode data through RF signatures

**Tag Architecture:**

**Encoding scheme:**
- **5-bit system**: 1 large resonator (character prefix) + 4 small resonators (numeric code)
- **Example ID "A1111"**: Apple-shaped "A" resonator + four active bit resonators
- **Frequency range**: 2-15 GHz
- **Resonator types**: U-shaped and meandered structures
- **Ink**: Ag nanoparticle (single layer for maximum resonance)

**Bit encoding:**
- **Bit "1"**: Resonator present ‚Üí Notch (dip) at specific frequency
- **Bit "0"**: Resonator absent ‚Üí No notch at that frequency
- **Multiple frequencies**: Different bit positions = unique spectral signature

**Fabrication:**
- Compact meandered trace shapes for size efficiency
- Printed using highly conductive Ag nanoparticle ink
- Thin and flexible (negligible impact on catheter mechanics)
- No power required (passive operation)

**Characterization:**

**Simulation:** CST Studio (plane wave interrogation, 15 cm range)

**Experimental setup:**
- Anechoic chamber testing
- Vivaldi UWB antenna (1.5-15 GHz)
- Vector network analyzer (Keysight N5230A PNA-L)
- Distance: approximately 15 cm from tag
- Monostatic radar cross section (RCS) measurement

**Signal Processing:**
- Clear frequency notches at designed resonant frequencies
- Slight frequency shift in measured vs simulated (due to ink conductivity, flexing)
- Distinct signatures for different tag codes

**Machine Learning Decoder:**

**Algorithm:** Multi-layer perceptron (MLP) neural network

**Training:**
- Input: RCS spectrum with notch depths at resonant frequencies

## Blockchain Integration for Device Traceability

### The Medical Device Tracking Problem

**Recent Catheter Recalls (2025):**
- **Medline arterial catheters**: Detachable hub material risking embolism
- **Johnson & Johnson Varipulse**: Elevated stroke rates
- **Medtronic aortic root cannulas**: Loose material hazards
- **Becton Dickinson Rotarex**: Fractures causing 30 injuries, 4 deaths

**Critical Need**: Secure, immutable tracking from manufacturing through disposal

### Blockchain-Based Solution

**System Architecture:**

**Manufacturing:**
- Each catheter assigned unique chipless RFID tag ID during printing
- ID logged to Ethereum blockchain-based secure database
- Immutable record created at point of manufacture
- Lot number, size, sterilization history, production date recorded

**Supply Chain:**
- Wireless scanning at each checkpoint
- Verification against blockchain ledger
- Prevents counterfeit devices entering supply chain
- Tracks distribution from factory to hospital

**Clinical Use:**

**Pre-procedure:**
- Nurse scans catheter ID after opening package
- System pulls manufacturing data, verifies authenticity
- Checks if device is part of recalled batch
- Confirms correct size/type for intended patient

**During procedure:**
- Used catheter ID logged with patient records
- Enables post-operative tracking
- Links device performance to patient outcomes

**Post-procedure:**
- Lifecycle data recorded
- Future recall notifications possible
- Adverse event correlation

**Advantages:**
- **Security**: Blockchain immutability prevents tampering
- **Transparency**: Complete device history accessible
- **Efficiency**: Rapid verification and recall management
- **Compliance**: Regulatory traceability requirements met
- **Cost**: Chipless tags add negligible manufacturing cost
- **Integration**: No interference with catheter sensing functions

- Current: 5-20 mm/s (relatively slow)
- Impact: Full catheter printing takes 15-45 minutes
- **Future solutions:**
  - Multi-nozzle parallel printing
  - Improved ink curing (UV-curable formulations)
  - Optimized toolpath algorithms

**Height Sensing:**
- Requires optically accessible surfaces
- Challenge with transparent or highly reflective catheters
- **Alternative sensors**: LiDAR, inductive, capacitive distance sensing
- Easy integration into LIMR platform (only height input changes)

**Data Capacity:**
- RFID tags currently limited to 4-bit codes
- **Future enhancements:**
